Losing an erection before penetration is a common experience often caused by performance anxiety, physical health issues like poor blood flow, or a loss of stimulation during the transition to intercourse. This specific challenge, sometimes called "maintenance dysfunction," occurs when the body cannot sustain the blood pressure required for a firm erection. While frustrating, it is frequently treatable once the underlying cause—whether psychological, vascular, or lifestyle-related—is identified.

## The Role of Performance Anxiety
One of the most frequent reasons men find that **my erection go away** right before penetration is performance anxiety. When you feel pressured to perform, your brain may trigger the sympathetic nervous system, also known as the "fight or flight" response.

According to [Harvard Health](https://www.health.harvard.edu/mens-health/the-psychological-side-of-erectile-dysfunction), the release of adrenaline during stress causes blood vessels to constrict. This is the physiological opposite of what is needed for an erection, which requires blood vessels to dilate. This can lead to a phenomenon called "spectatoring," where a man becomes an over-analytical observer of his own sexual performance, further increasing stress and causing the erection to subside.

## Physical and Vascular Causes
If you find that **my erection go** soft consistently, it may be due to an underlying physical issue. For an erection to stay firm, blood must not only flow into the penis but also remain trapped there.

- **Venous Leak:** This occurs when the veins in the penis cannot properly compress to keep blood from flowing out. Even if you can achieve an erection initially, the "trap" doesn't hold, leading to a loss of firmness before or during penetration.
- **Blood Flow Issues:** Conditions like hypertension, high cholesterol, or diabetes can damage the lining of the blood vessels, making it harder to maintain the necessary pressure.
- **Age-Related Changes:** While ED can happen at any time, the primary causes often shift as you get older. You can learn more about these shifts in our guide to [erectile dysfunction by age](/blog/ed-causes-by-age-20s-30s-40s-50s-60s).

The [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/10035-erectile-dysfunction) notes that vascular issues are among the most common physical causes of maintenance-related ED.

## Contextual and Lifestyle Factors
Sometimes the reason an erection fades is purely contextual. The transition from foreplay to penetration often involves a pause in direct physical stimulation—such as when reaching for a condom or changing positions.

- **Condom Use:** For some, the interruption or the reduced sensitivity of a condom can cause a loss of focus. If you find [i lose my erection](/blog/why-do-i-lose-my-erection-with-a-condom) specifically during this step, it may be a combination of reduced sensation and anxiety.
- **Distractions:** Environmental factors, such as noise or a wandering mind, can break the "arousal loop" between the brain and the body.
- **Fatigue and Alcohol:** Both can dull the nervous system's response, making it harder for the body to maintain the signals required for a firm erection.

If you are experiencing a pattern where [i get hard but](/blog/why-do-i-get-hard-but-can-t-maintain-it) can’t stay that way, it is helpful to track when it happens to see if there is a specific trigger.

## When to Speak With a Clinician
It is important to treat any recurring erection issue as a "check engine light" for your overall health. For a deeper look at the various medical and psychological treatments available, see our [complete guide erectile dysfunction](/blog/complete-guide-erectile-dysfunction).

**Important Safety Note:**
Before starting any new treatment for ED, you must consult a healthcare professional. PDE5 inhibitors (the class of medication used for ED) are strictly contraindicated for men taking nitrates (often prescribed for chest pain) or those with unstable cardiovascular disease. Combining these can lead to a dangerous drop in blood pressure. 

Seek urgent medical care if you experience:
- Chest pain during sexual activity.
- An erection lasting longer than four hours (priapism).
- Sudden vision or hearing loss.
- Symptoms of a heart attack or stroke.
