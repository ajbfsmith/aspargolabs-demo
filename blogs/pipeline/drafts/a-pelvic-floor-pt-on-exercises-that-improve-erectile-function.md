When most men think about treating erectile dysfunction (ED), they think about chemistry—pills, hormones, and blood flow. While those are critical components, there is a mechanical side to sexual health that often goes overlooked: the pelvic floor.

The pelvic floor is a hammock-like group of muscles that supports the bladder and bowel and plays a direct role in erectile rigidity and duration. To better understand how physical therapy can impact performance, we sat down with an expert **pelvic floor pt on** the clinical connection between muscle strength and sexual function.

## The Role of the Pelvic Floor in Erections

**Accelerate Health: Why does a pelvic floor PT care about erectile function?**

**Dr. Vance:** Most people associate pelvic floor physical therapy (PFPT) with women’s health or incontinence, but the anatomy is just as vital for men. Two specific muscles—the ischiocavernosus and the bulbocavernosus—are responsible for the mechanical side of an erection. 

The ischiocavernosus muscle surrounds the base of the penis. When it contracts, it compresses the veins that allow blood to leave the penis. This "trapping" of blood is what maintains rigidity. If these muscles are weak or poorly coordinated, you might achieve an erection but lose it quickly—a condition often referred to as "venous leak."

**Accelerate Health: What does the research say about the effectiveness of these exercises?**

**Dr. Vance:** The evidence is quite compelling. A landmark study published in [BJU International](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15833065/) found that 40% of men with ED regained normal erectile function after six months of pelvic floor muscle training, and an additional 35% saw significant improvement. This suggests that for many, the "hardware" is just as important as the "software."

## Exercises to Improve Erectile Function

**Accelerate Health: What are the primary exercises a pelvic floor PT on this journey would recommend?**

**Dr. Vance:** It’s not just about "doing more Kegels." In fact, some men have a "hypertonic" or overly tight pelvic floor, which can also cause ED by restricting blood flow. We focus on three main pillars:

### 1. Targeted Kegels (Contraction)
The goal is to isolate the pelvic floor without engaging the glutes or abdominals. 
- **The Move:** Imagine you are trying to stop the flow of urine or prevent passing gas. 
- **The Cadence:** Hold for 3–5 seconds, then relax for 5 seconds. Repeat 10 times, three times a day.
- **Why it works:** Strengthening these muscles improves the pressure required to maintain an erection.

### 2. Reverse Kegels (Relaxation)
For men whose ED is linked to pelvic pain or high muscle tension, we focus on lengthening.
- **The Move:** Using diaphragmatic breathing, "push" the pelvic floor downward gently, as if starting a bowel movement.
- **Why it works:** It reduces tension that can prematurely trigger the nervous system's "fight or flight" response, which kills an erection.

### 3. Diaphragmatic Breathing
The diaphragm and the pelvic floor move together. Deep, "belly" breathing helps coordinate these muscles and reduces the stress hormones that often contribute to [erectile dysfunction by age](/blog/ed-causes-by-age-20s-30s-40s-50s-60s).

## Important Safety Considerations

While pelvic floor exercises are generally safe, ED can be a symptom of underlying cardiovascular disease. Before starting any new exercise or medication regimen:

- **Consult a clinician:** If you have unstable cardiovascular disease or experience chest pain, speak with a doctor immediately.
- **Nitrate Warning:** PDE5 inhibitors (like sildenafil) are strictly contraindicated for men taking nitrates (often prescribed for chest pain) or guanylate cyclase stimulators, as this can cause a life-threatening drop in blood pressure.
- **Seek Emergency Care:** If you experience an erection lasting longer than four hours (priapism), sudden vision loss, or sudden hearing loss, seek urgent medical attention.
