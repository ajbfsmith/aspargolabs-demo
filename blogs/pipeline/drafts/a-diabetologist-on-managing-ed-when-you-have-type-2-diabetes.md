Managing erectile dysfunction (ED) is a common challenge for men living with Type 2 diabetes. Because diabetes affects both the vascular and nervous systems, the physiological hurdles to achieving an erection can be more complex than they are for the general population.

## The Connection Between Blood Sugar and Sexual Health

**Accelerate Health: Why is erectile dysfunction so much more prevalent in men with Type 2 diabetes?**

**Dr. Thorne:** It comes down to the "triad" of diabetic complications: vascular damage, neuropathy, and hormonal shifts. High blood glucose levels over time can damage the lining of the small blood vessels (endothelium). This impairs the release of nitric oxide, the chemical signal required for the smooth muscles in the penis to relax and allow blood flow.

Furthermore, [diabetic neuropathy](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/urologic-diseases/erectile-dysfunction/symptoms-causes) can damage the nerves that trigger an erection. When you combine these physical factors with the psychological stress of managing a chronic illness, it creates a "perfect storm" for ED. Statistics suggest that men with diabetes are three times more likely to experience ED than those without it.

**Accelerate Health: Does improving blood sugar control reverse the damage?**

**Dr. Thorne:** Management is key, but "reversal" is a strong word. Maintaining an A1c within your target range can certainly prevent further damage to the nerves and blood vessels. In some cases, better glycemic control improves energy levels and testosterone balance, which helps. However, once significant vascular damage has occurred, lifestyle changes alone may not be enough to restore full function. This is where clinical interventions become necessary.

## Navigating Treatment Options

**Accelerate Health: What does the typical treatment path look like for a diabetic patient?**

**Dr. Thorne:** We usually start with a combination of lifestyle modification and oral medications. Because diabetes accelerates vascular aging, we often see [erectile dysfunction by age](/blog/ed-causes-by-age-20s-30s-40s-50s-60s) milestones occurring much earlier in diabetic patients.

Treatment often includes:
- **PDE5 Inhibitors:** The first-line therapy for most men.
- **Lifestyle Adjustments:** Weight management and cardiovascular exercise to improve endothelial function.
- **Hormone Therapy:** Checking for low testosterone, which is common in Type 2 diabetes.
- **Advanced Interventions:** If oral medications fail, we look at injections or vacuum devices.

**Accelerate Health: Many men with diabetes find that traditional pills are inconsistent. Why is that?**

**Dr. Thorne:** Absorption can be an issue. Many people with diabetes suffer from gastroparesis, a condition where the stomach empties slowly. This can delay the onset of a traditional pill or make its effects unpredictable. This is why delivery innovation is so important.

## Safety and Cardiovascular Considerations

**Accelerate Health: What safety warnings are most critical for men with diabetes?**

**Dr. Thorne:** This is the most important part of the conversation. Diabetes is often comorbid with cardiovascular disease. **PDE5 inhibitors, including HEZKUE, are strictly contraindicated for men taking nitrates** (often prescribed for chest pain/angina). Taking these together can cause a life-threatening drop in blood pressure.

Men with unstable cardiovascular disease, or those who experience shortness of breath or chest pain during physical exertion, must speak with a clinician before trying any ED medication. If you experience symptoms of a heart attack, sudden vision loss, or a prolonged erection lasting more than four hours (priapism), you must seek urgent medical care immediately.
