Erectile dysfunction (ED) is one of the most common health conditions affecting men worldwide, yet it is often discussed as if it were a monolithic experience. In reality, the demographics of ED, who is most affected and why, reveal a complex interplay of age, underlying health conditions, and lifestyle factors. Understanding these patterns is essential for identifying the right treatment approach and recognizing that ED is a clinical medical issue rather than a personal failing.

## The Role of Age in ED Prevalence

While ED can affect men at any stage of adulthood, age remains the strongest predictor of prevalence. Research historically suggests that approximately 50% of men between the ages of 40 and 70 experience some degree of ED. According to the [National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases (NIDDK)](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/urologic-diseases/erectile-dysfunction/definition-facts), the likelihood of experiencing ED increases significantly with each decade of life.

However, the nature of ED often changes as men age. In older populations, the cause is frequently physiological, often related to the natural decline in testosterone or the hardening of arteries (atherosclerosis) which restricts blood flow. You can learn more about these specific transitions in our guide to [erectile dysfunction by age](/blog/ed-causes-by-age-20s-30s-40s-50s-60s).

## The Rising Trend Among Younger Men

One of the most significant shifts in ED demographics over the last decade is the increasing rate of diagnosis in men under 40. While older men may face ED due to long-term vascular issues, younger men are more likely to experience ED driven by psychological factors, such as performance anxiety, or lifestyle-related issues.

Recent data indicates that [one in four men seeking treatment for ED is under the age of 40](https://www.jsm.jsexmed.org/article/S1743-6095(15)30428-8/fulltext). This shift is partly attributed to increased awareness and a reduction in the stigma surrounding men's sexual health, but it also reflects modern stressors. Furthermore, [ed rates have changed](/blog/how-ed-rates-have-changed-since-covid-2020-2026-data-review) in recent years, with environmental and psychological stressors playing a larger role in the global demographic landscape.

## Comorbidities: Health Conditions That Increase Risk

ED is often a "canary in the coal mine" for other systemic health issues. Certain demographics defined by chronic health conditions are at a much higher risk:

- **Diabetes:** Men with diabetes are two to three times more likely to develop ED than those without the condition. High blood sugar can damage the small blood vessels and nerves responsible for achieving an erection.
- **Cardiovascular Disease:** Because erections depend on healthy blood flow, ED is frequently an early warning sign of heart disease or hypertension. The [American Heart Association](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000670) notes that vascular ED and coronary artery disease share the same underlying pathology.
- **Obesity:** Men with a high Body Mass Index (BMI) often face hormonal imbalances and increased inflammation, both of which contribute to ED.

For a deeper look at how these conditions interact with sexual health, see our [complete guide erectile dysfunction](/blog/complete-guide-erectile-dysfunction).

## Racial and Socioeconomic Factors

Demographic data also suggests disparities in ED prevalence and treatment access across different racial and ethnic groups. Some studies have indicated that Black and Hispanic men may experience higher rates of ED, often because they have higher statistical prevalences of risk factors like hypertension and type 2 diabetes.

Socioeconomic status also plays a role in how ED is managed. Men with better access to healthcare are more likely to receive early intervention for the underlying causes of ED. Regardless of your demographic, ED treatments like sildenafil are medical products that require professional oversight.

**Do not take HEZKUE or any PDE5 inhibitor if you take nitrates (often prescribed for chest pain) or use "poppers" (amyl nitrite), as this can cause a dangerous drop in blood pressure.**

Men with unstable cardiovascular disease, severe liver or kidney impairment, or those who have recently suffered a stroke or heart attack must speak with a clinician before considering ED medication. Seek urgent medical care if you experience:
- Chest pain during or after sexual activity.
- An erection lasting more than four hours (priapism).
- Sudden vision or hearing loss.
