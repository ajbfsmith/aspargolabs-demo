While erectile dysfunction (ED) is often associated with aging, it can start at any age. Statistically, the **age ED typically starts** to become a common clinical concern is 40. Research from the landmark Massachusetts Male Aging Study indicates that approximately 40% of men experience some degree of ED by age 40, with the prevalence increasing by about 10% for every decade thereafter. However, it is not exclusive to older populations; nearly 1 in 4 men seeking their first treatment for ED are now under the age of 40.

## Understanding the "40% at 40" Rule

For decades, the medical community has used the "40% at 40" benchmark to describe when the **age ED typically** begins to manifest for a significant portion of the population. This correlation exists because the physiological factors required for an erection—blood flow, nerve signaling, and hormone levels—often begin to shift as a man enters middle age.

By age 70, the prevalence of ED reaches approximately 70%. While these statistics suggest that ED is a common part of aging, it is important to note that ED is not considered an inevitable part of getting older. It is frequently a symptom of an underlying health condition, such as cardiovascular disease or diabetes, rather than a standalone result of the passage of time.

## Why ED Can Start Earlier

While 40 is the statistical average for onset, many men experience symptoms much earlier. In younger men (those in their 20s and 30s), the causes are often different than those found in older men.

- **Psychological Factors:** Performance anxiety, stress, and depression are leading causes of ED in younger populations.
- **Lifestyle Choices:** Smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, and a sedentary lifestyle can impair vascular health early in life.
- **Underlying Conditions:** Early-onset type 2 diabetes or obesity can lead to ED symptoms well before age 40.

Understanding [what a normal erection is supposed to feel like](/blog/what-is-a-normal-erection-supposed-to-feel-like) can help you identify early changes in firmness or duration, which may be the first sign of a vascular or psychological issue.

## The Role of Vascular Health

The most common reason the **age ED typically starts** in the 40s and 50s is the gradual decline in vascular health. The arteries that supply blood to the penis are significantly smaller than those supplying the heart. Consequently, ED is often the "canary in the coal mine," serving as an early warning sign of atherosclerosis (hardening of the arteries) or hypertension.

Because of this connection, any man experiencing a new onset of ED should consult a healthcare provider to screen for cardiovascular risks. ## Safety and Medical Considerations

Before starting any treatment for ED, it is vital to understand that medications in the PDE5 inhibitor class (such as sildenafil) are not safe for everyone.

**Important Safety Information:**
- PDE5 inhibitors are strictly contraindicated with the use of nitrates (often prescribed for chest pain) and certain recreational drugs called "poppers." Combining these can cause a life-threatening drop in blood pressure.
- Men with unstable cardiovascular disease, recent history of stroke or heart attack, or severe liver/kidney impairment must speak with a clinician before use.
- Seek urgent medical care if you experience chest pain, symptoms of a heart attack, sudden vision or hearing loss, or a painful erection lasting longer than four hours (priapism).
