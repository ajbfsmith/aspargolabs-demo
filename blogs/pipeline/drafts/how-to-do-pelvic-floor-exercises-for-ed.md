Pelvic floor exercises, often called Kegels, can improve erectile dysfunction (ED) by strengthening the muscles that support blood flow to the penis and help maintain rigidity. Research indicates that targeted training of the bulbocavernosus and ischiocavernosus muscles can lead to significant improvements in erectile function within three to six months. These exercises work by enhancing the "muscle pump" that keeps blood trapped in the penis during an erection. While not a replacement for medical treatment in all cases, pelvic floor exercises are a clinically supported, non-invasive way to support sexual health.

## Why Pelvic Floor Exercises Help with ED

The pelvic floor is a layer of muscles that supports the bladder and bowel and plays a critical role in sexual function. In men, two specific muscles, the bulbocavernosus and the ischiocavernosus, are responsible for compressing the veins that drain blood away from the penis. When these muscles are strong, they help maintain the pressure required for a firm erection.

A landmark study published in [BJU International](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15833064/) found that 40% of men with ED regained normal erectile function after six months of pelvic floor exercises, while an additional 35.5% showed significant improvement. For more context on how lifestyle and physical factors contribute to sexual health, you can read our [complete guide erectile dysfunction](/blog/complete-guide-erectile-dysfunction).

## How to Identify Your Pelvic Floor Muscles

The most difficult part of pelvic floor exercises is identifying the correct muscles without engaging your abdominals, buttocks, or thighs. There are two common ways to locate them:

1.  **The "Stop the Flow" Method:** The next time you are urinating, try to stop or slow the flow of urine mid-stream. The muscles you use to do this are your pelvic floor muscles. (Note: Do not do this regularly while urinating, as it can lead to urinary tract issues; use it only once to find the muscle).
2.  **The "Hold Gas" Method:** Imagine you are trying to prevent yourself from passing gas. Squeeze the muscles around your anus. You should feel a lifting sensation in the pelvic area.

According to [Harvard Health](https://www.health.harvard.edu/mens-health/pelvic-floor-exercises-for-men), if you feel a slight pulling-in of your penis and a lifting of your scrotum, you are likely engaging the correct muscles.

## A Step-by-Step Pelvic Floor Routine

Once you have identified the muscles, follow this routine to build strength over time:

1.  **The Squeeze:** Tighten your pelvic floor muscles and hold for three seconds.
2.  **The Relax:** Release the muscles completely and count to three.
3.  **The Repetition:** Repeat this cycle 10 times.
4.  **The Frequency:** Aim to perform a set of 10 repetitions three times per day.

As your muscles get stronger, you can increase the hold time to 5 or 10 seconds. It is important to breathe normally throughout the exercises and avoid holding your breath or tensing your stomach. Consistency is key; most men do not see results for at least 4 to 6 weeks, with maximum benefit occurring after several months.

## When to Combine Exercises with Other Treatments

Pelvic floor exercises are often most effective when used as part of a comprehensive approach to ED. This may include lifestyle changes, such as weight management and smoking cessation, or the use of clinical treatments. If you are [taking ed medication first](/blog/first-time-ed-medication-what-to-expect), you may find that improved muscle tone helps the medication work more effectively by supporting the physical mechanisms of an erection.

For those who need more immediate support while working on their long-term pelvic health, some men also [use vacuum erection device](/blog/how-to-use-a-vacuum-erection-device) therapy or pharmacological options. If you are unsure which path is right for you, it is helpful to [talk doctor about ed](/blog/how-to-talk-to-your-doctor-about-ed-with-exact-scripts) to rule out underlying cardiovascular issues.

## Important Safety Considerations

While pelvic floor exercises are safe for most men, ED can sometimes be an early warning sign of cardiovascular disease. It is essential to consult a healthcare provider if you experience sudden ED or if you have symptoms like chest pain or shortness of breath.

**Safety Warning:** If you are prescribed PDE5 inhibitors (such as sildenafil), they are strictly contraindicated for men taking nitrates for chest pain or those with unstable cardiovascular disease. Combining these can lead to a dangerous drop in blood pressure. Always seek urgent medical care for an erection lasting longer than four hours (priapism) or sudden changes in vision or hearing.
