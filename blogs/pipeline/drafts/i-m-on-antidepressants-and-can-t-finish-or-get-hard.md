If you are on antidepressants and find you can't get hard or reach orgasm, you are experiencing a common side effect known as antidepressant-induced sexual dysfunction. Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) and Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs) can interfere with the chemical signals required for arousal and climax. Research suggests that between 30% and 70% of people taking these medications experience some form of sexual side effect, ranging from decreased libido to erectile dysfunction (ED) and delayed ejaculation.

## Why Antidepressants Affect Sexual Function

Antidepressants work by altering neurotransmitters in the brain to improve mood. However, these same chemicals play a critical role in the sexual response cycle.

The most common culprits are SSRIs (like fluoxetine or sertraline) and SNRIs (like venlafaxine). These medications increase the levels of serotonin in the brain. While higher serotonin helps stabilize mood, it can also inhibit the release of dopamine and norepinephrine—neurotransmitters that are essential for feeling desire and achieving arousal. Furthermore, serotonin can interfere with the production of nitric oxide, the chemical responsible for relaxing the blood vessels in the penis to allow for an erection.

It is a complex cycle because depression itself often causes sexual issues. Distinguishing between [psychological erectile dysfunction anxiety](/blog/psychological-erectile-dysfunction-guide) and medication-induced side effects is a vital first step in finding a solution.

## The "Can't Finish" Problem: Anorgasmia

One of the most frustrating side effects of being on antidepressants is anorgasmia, or the inability to reach orgasm despite adequate stimulation. Because SSRIs dampen the sensory signals and the "reward" pathways in the brain, the threshold required to reach climax becomes much higher.

According to the [Mayo Clinic](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/depression/in-depth/antidepressants/art-20048273), this can lead to a "numbing" sensation or a significant delay in ejaculation. For many men, this creates a secondary layer of performance anxiety, which further complicates their ability to stay hard.

## The "Can't Get Hard" Problem: Erectile Dysfunction

Erectile dysfunction is another frequent side effect. To achieve an erection, the nervous system must send signals to the blood vessels in the penis to dilate. Antidepressants can blunt these neurological signals.

If you find that your ability to get an erection has changed specifically since starting a new prescription, the medication is likely the primary factor. However, age and underlying health can also play a role. You can learn more about how these factors intersect in our guide to [erectile dysfunction by age](/blog/ed-causes-by-age-20s-30s-40s-50s-60s).

## How to Manage Sexual Side Effects

You should never stop taking your antidepressant medication without consulting your doctor, as "cold turkey" cessation can lead to severe withdrawal symptoms or a relapse of depression. Instead, consider these medically supervised strategies:

- **Wait and see:** In some cases, sexual side effects may diminish after the first few months as the body adjusts to the medication.
- **Dosage adjustment:** A physician may be able to lower your dose to a level that maintains your mental health while reducing sexual interference.
- **Switching medications:** Some antidepressants, such as bupropion (Wellbutrin) or mirtazapine (Remeron), have a much lower incidence of sexual side effects because they do not primarily target serotonin.
- **Adding a PDE5 inhibitor:** Medications like sildenafil (the active ingredient in HEZKUE) are often prescribed alongside antidepressants to help manage ED. According to a study published in the [Journal of the American Medical Association (JAMA)](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/196854), sildenafil has been shown to effectively treat ED in men whose dysfunction was induced by SSRIs.

## Important Safety Information

Before considering any treatment for erectile dysfunction, it is essential to understand that PDE5 inhibitors are not safe for everyone. You must speak with a clinician before use, especially if you have unstable cardiovascular disease or a history of heart problems.

**Do not take sildenafil or any PDE5 inhibitor if you take nitrates** (often prescribed for chest pain) or use recreational "poppers" containing amyl nitrate, as this can cause a life-threatening drop in blood pressure. Seek urgent medical care if you experience chest pain, symptoms of a heart attack, sudden vision or hearing loss, or an erection lasting longer than four hours (priapism).
