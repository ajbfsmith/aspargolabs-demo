If you find yourself in a situation where you "got hard once" but then struggled to achieve a second erection shortly after, or if you had a successful encounter one night but failed the next, you are likely not experiencing chronic erectile dysfunction (ED). Clinical ED is typically defined as a persistent or recurrent inability to achieve or maintain an erection sufficient for satisfactory sexual performance. A single instance of difficulty is often a result of temporary physiological or psychological factors rather than a long-term medical condition.

## Understanding the Refractory Period

One of the most common reasons a man might say "I got hard once but not again" during a single sexual encounter is the biological refractory period. This is the recovery phase after ejaculation during which it is physically impossible for a man to achieve another erection, regardless of the amount of stimulation.

According to the [International Society for Sexual Medicine](https://www.issm.info/sexual-health-qa/what-is-the-male-refractory-period/), the length of this period varies significantly based on age and health. For a young man, it might last only a few minutes; for older men, it can last several hours or even days. If you are comparing your current recovery time to how you performed in your teens or early twenties, you may simply be experiencing a natural shift in your [erectile dysfunction by age](/blog/ed-causes-by-age-20s-30s-40s-50s-60s).

## Psychological Factors and Performance Anxiety

If you were able to get hard initially but lost the erection or couldn't "go again" due to worry, you may be dealing with performance anxiety. This is a leading cause of [psychological erectile dysfunction anxiety](/blog/psychological-erectile-dysfunction-guide). 

When the brain perceives stress or pressure to perform, it triggers the sympathetic nervous system (the "fight or flight" response). This releases adrenaline, which constricts blood vessels and pulls blood away from the extremities—including the penis—making an erection difficult to maintain. If you find that [i get hard but](/blog/i-can-get-hard-but-not-with-my-partner-is-this-ed) lose it the moment things get serious, the issue is likely mental rather than a physical failure of the circulatory system.

## Common Temporary Triggers

Several lifestyle factors can cause a "one-off" failure even if your system is otherwise healthy:

- **Alcohol Consumption:** While alcohol may lower inhibitions, it is a central nervous system depressant. It can dull sensation and dehydrate the body, leading to what is colloquially known as "whiskey dick."
- **Fatigue:** Sexual arousal requires significant energy. If you are exhausted, your body may prioritize rest over sexual function.
- **New Medications:** Certain medications, including some antidepressants or blood pressure drugs, can interfere with the signals required for an erection.
- **Stress:** High levels of cortisol from work or personal stress can interfere with the testosterone and dopamine levels needed for arousal.

## When Does It Become "Real" ED?

While an isolated incident is usually not a cause for alarm, you should pay attention if the pattern becomes consistent. Medical professionals generally look for symptoms that persist for three months or longer. If you notice that [my erection isn't as](/blog/my-erection-isn-t-as-firm-as-it-used-to-be) firm as it used to be on a regular basis, it may be time to consult a clinician.

According to the [Mayo Clinic](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/erectile-dysfunction/symptoms-causes/syc-20355776), ED can sometimes be an early warning sign of underlying health issues like heart disease, diabetes, or high blood pressure. For a deeper look at the various physical and mental triggers, refer to our [complete guide erectile dysfunction](/blog/complete-guide-erectile-dysfunction).

## Safety and Next Steps

If you are considering medical treatment for ED, it is vital to speak with a healthcare provider first. **PDE5 inhibitors (like sildenafil) are strictly contraindicated for men taking nitrates for chest pain or those with unstable cardiovascular disease.** Combining these can lead to a dangerous drop in blood pressure. You should also seek immediate medical attention if you experience chest pain during sex, sudden vision or hearing loss, or an erection lasting longer than four hours (priapism).
