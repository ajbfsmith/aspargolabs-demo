When a man visits a urologist for erectile dysfunction (ED), the diagnosis often begins with a conversation about symptoms and medical history. However, to understand the "why" behind the symptoms, specialists often turn to objective data. Because an erection is primarily a hemodynamic (blood flow) event, doctors use specific diagnostic tools to see exactly how blood moves through the penis.

Understanding how **doctors measure penile blood** flow can help you move from guesswork to a targeted treatment plan. The gold standard for this assessment is the Penile Duplex Doppler Ultrasound (PDDU), a procedure that provides real-time images and measurements of the vascular system at work.

## The Gold Standard: Penile Duplex Doppler Ultrasound

A Penile Duplex Doppler Ultrasound is more than just a standard imaging test. It combines traditional ultrasound, which shows the structure of the tissues, with Doppler technology, which measures the speed and direction of blood flow.

The procedure typically involves two phases:
1.  **The Flaccid State:** An initial scan is performed while the penis is flaccid to check for structural issues like plaques (common in Peyronie’s disease) or calcification.
2.  **The Stimulated State:** To see how the vessels perform during an erection, a doctor or technician will administer a small injection of a vasoactive medication (usually prostaglandin E1 or a "Trimix" blend) into the side of the penis. This medication bypasses the nervous system to relax the smooth muscles and dilate the arteries, mimicking natural arousal.

During this second phase, the specialist focuses on **the cavernosal artery**. There are two cavernosal arteries, one running through the center of each corpus cavernosum (the sponge-like chambers that fill with blood). These are the primary vessels responsible for delivering the volume of blood required for rigidity.

## Decoding the Numbers: PSV, EDV, and RI

As the medication takes effect, the ultrasound machine records several key metrics. These numbers tell the doctor whether the issue is getting blood into the penis, keeping it there, or a combination of both.

### Peak Systolic Velocity (PSV)
PSV measures the maximum speed at which blood rushes into the penis through the cavernosal arteries during the contraction of the heart (systole). 
-   **Normal:** A **PSV >25 cm/s** is generally considered the baseline for healthy arterial inflow, though many specialists prefer to see values above 30 or 35 cm/s for optimal function.
-   **Arterial Insufficiency:** If the PSV is consistently below 25 cm/s, it suggests that the arteries are narrowed or hardened—a condition known as arterial insufficiency. This is often linked to systemic issues like atherosclerosis or [chronic inflammation ed systemic](/blog/inflammation-erectile-dysfunction).

### End-Diastolic Velocity (EDV)
EDV measures the speed of blood flow between heartbeats (diastole). In a healthy erection, the veins should be compressed so tightly that blood cannot escape, meaning the EDV should drop significantly.
-   **Normal:** An EDV of less than 5 cm/s is considered normal.
-   **Venous Leak:** If the EDV remains high (above 5 cm/s) even when the arteries are providing plenty of blood, it indicates that the "trapdoor" mechanism is failing. This is known as [venous leakage ed difficult](/blog/venous-leak-ed), where blood flows in correctly but drains out too fast to maintain rigidity.

### Resistive Index (RI)
The RI is a calculated value that represents the overall resistance to blood flow within the penis. It is found using the formula: *(PSV - EDV) / PSV*.
-   **Normal:** An RI greater than 0.8 is typically the goal. A high RI indicates that the internal pressure of the penis is high enough to resist further inflow, which is a hallmark of a firm, stable erection.

## Low Flow vs. Venous Leakage: What It Means for You

By looking at these numbers together, a clinician can categorize the ED into specific types, which dictates the most effective treatment path.

### Arterial Insufficiency (Low Inflow)
When the PSV is low, the problem is "supply." The vessels are not delivering enough blood to fill the chambers. This is frequently the first sign of broader cardiovascular health issues. In these cases, [pde5 inhibitors work complete](/blog/how-pde5-inhibitors-work) by helping the existing vessels relax more effectively, though lifestyle changes like diet and exercise are often required to improve long-term vascular health.

### Venous Leakage (Poor Outflow Control)
When the EDV is high, the problem is "retention." The veins are not closing properly. This can be caused by damage to the tunica albuginea (the sheath surrounding the erectile tissue) or by the smooth muscles failing to relax enough to compress the veins. Venous leak is often more difficult to treat with standard oral medications alone and may require specialized delivery formats or different clinical interventions.

### Mixed Vasculogenic ED
Many men show a combination of both low inflow and high outflow. This "mixed" profile is common in long-term diabetes or advanced vascular disease, where both the arteries and the venous-occlusive mechanism have been affected.

## Treatment Implications

The results of a blood flow test are essential for a [complete guide erectile dysfunction](/blog/complete-guide-erectile-dysfunction) management plan. If the test shows healthy blood flow (normal PSV and EDV) but the patient still struggles with erections, the doctor may look toward psychological factors, nerve damage, or hormonal imbalances.

However, if the numbers confirm a vascular cause, the focus shifts to improving hemodynamics. For those with mild-to-moderate arterial issues, medications that increase nitric oxide availability are the first line of defense. The speed and consistency of how these medications are delivered can significantly impact the user experience, especially for men who want a more predictable onset.

***

**Medical Disclaimer:** PDE5 inhibitors are contraindicated for men taking nitrates (often prescribed for chest pain) or guanylate cyclase stimulators. If you have unstable cardiovascular disease or experience symptoms like chest pain, sudden vision loss, or an erection lasting longer than four hours (priapism), seek urgent medical care. Always consult with a clinician to ensure any ED treatment is safe for your specific health profile.
