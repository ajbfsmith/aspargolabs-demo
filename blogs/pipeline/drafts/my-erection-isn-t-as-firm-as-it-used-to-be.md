If you have noticed that **my erection isn't as** firm as it used to be, you are not alone; a change in rigidity is often the first sign of erectile dysfunction (ED). While occasional fluctuations in firmness are normal, a consistent decrease in hardness typically signals changes in vascular health, hormonal shifts, or psychological factors like stress. It is rarely a sign that you are "broken," but it is a clinical signal that your body’s blood delivery system may need support.

## Understanding the Erection Hardness Score (EHS)

Medical professionals often use the Erection Hardness Score (EHS) to help men categorize their symptoms. If you feel **my erection isn't** reaching its full potential, it helps to identify where you fall on this four-point scale:

1.  **Grade 1:** The penis is larger than normal but not hard.
2.  **Grade 2:** The penis is hard, but not hard enough for penetration.
3.  **Grade 3:** The penis is hard enough for penetration, but not completely firm.
4.  **Grade 4:** The penis is completely hard and fully rigid.

Many men seeking help find themselves at Grade 3. While penetration is possible, the lack of full rigidity can lead to reduced sensation or anxiety during intimacy. For a deeper look at how these symptoms progress, see our [complete guide erectile dysfunction](/blog/complete-guide-erectile-dysfunction).

## Why My Erection Isn't as Firm Anymore

The mechanics of an erection rely almost entirely on healthy blood flow. When you are aroused, the muscles in the penis relax, allowing blood to flow into the corpora cavernosa. If that blood cannot enter quickly enough, or if it "leaks" out too soon, firmness decreases.

### Vascular Health and Blood Flow
The most common reason for a loss of firmness is a change in vascular health. Conditions like high blood pressure, high cholesterol, or early-stage cardiovascular disease can narrow the small blood vessels in the penis long before they affect the larger vessels around the heart. According to the [Mayo Clinic](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/erectile-dysfunction/symptoms-causes/syc-20353176), ED can often be an early warning sign of underlying heart issues.

### Psychological Factors
Sometimes the issue isn't physical at all. Stress, performance anxiety, and depression can trigger the body's "fight or flight" response, which constricts blood vessels and makes it difficult to achieve or maintain a Grade 4 erection. If you find that you have firm erections upon waking but struggle during partner play, you may be experiencing [psychological erectile dysfunction anxiety](/blog/psychological-erectile-dysfunction-guide).

### Age and Hormones
As men age, testosterone levels naturally decline, and the tissues in the penis may become less elastic. However, age is not the only factor; lifestyle choices regarding diet, exercise, and smoking play a massive role in maintaining rigidity. You can learn more about how these factors change over time in our article on [erectile dysfunction by age](/blog/ed-causes-by-age-20s-30s-40s-50s-60s).

## When to Speak With a Clinician

If you notice that **my erection isn't as** reliable as it once was, it is important to consult a healthcare provider. This is especially true if the change is sudden or accompanied by other symptoms.

**Important Safety Note:** PDE5 inhibitors (the class of medication used to treat ED) are strictly contraindicated for men taking nitrates for chest pain or those using "poppers" (amyl nitrate). Combining these can cause a life-threatening drop in blood pressure. Men with unstable cardiovascular disease or those who have recently suffered a stroke or heart attack must speak with a clinician before seeking treatment.

Seek urgent medical care if you experience:
- Chest pain during sexual activity.
- An erection lasting longer than four hours (priapism).
- Sudden loss of vision or hearing.
