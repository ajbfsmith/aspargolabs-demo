ED medications actually make an erection possible by blocking an enzyme called phosphodiesterase type 5 (PDE5). By inhibiting this enzyme, the medications help maintain higher levels of cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP), a chemical that relaxes smooth muscle tissue in the penis. This relaxation allows blood vessels to widen and fill with the blood necessary to produce and sustain an erection. It is important to note that these medications are not "on" switches; they require natural sexual stimulation to initiate the chemical process.

## The Biological Chain Reaction

To understand how ED medications actually work, it helps to look at what happens in the body during sexual arousal. An erection is essentially a hemodynamic event—a change in blood flow—governed by the nervous system and chemical messengers.

1.  **Arousal:** When you become stimulated, your brain sends signals to the nerve endings in the penis.
2.  **Nitric Oxide Release:** These nerves release nitric oxide (NO).
3.  **The Messenger (cGMP):** Nitric oxide activates an enzyme that produces cGMP. This is the "key" that tells the smooth muscles in the corpora cavernosa (the sponge-like chambers in the penis) to relax.
4.  **Blood Inflow:** As the muscles relax, the arteries dilate, and blood rushes in. The pressure of this blood traps itself in the chambers, resulting in an erection.

According to the [National Institutes of Health](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK544360/), the primary role of PDE5 inhibitors like sildenafil is to prevent the breakdown of cGMP, ensuring that the signal for "muscle relaxation" stays active for longer.

## Why PDE5 is the "Off Switch"

Under normal circumstances, the body needs a way to end an erection. This is where the enzyme PDE5 comes in. Its job is to break down cGMP. Once cGMP is gone, the smooth muscles contract again, blood flows out, and the penis returns to a flaccid state.

For men with erectile dysfunction, the balance is often off. Either not enough cGMP is produced, or it is broken down too quickly by PDE5 before an erection can be sustained. ED medications actually work by sitting in the "pocket" of the PDE5 enzyme, physically blocking it from reaching the cGMP molecules. This allows the blood-flow-promoting chemicals to accumulate and do their job more effectively.

## The Necessity of Sexual Stimulation

A common misconception is that taking a pill or using a spray will result in an immediate, spontaneous erection. However, because PDE5 inhibitors work "downstream" of the initial signal, they cannot create an erection out of thin air. 

Without sexual arousal, the body does not release nitric oxide, and no cGMP is created. If there is no cGMP to protect, the medication has nothing to work on. This is why clinical guidance, such as that found in our [complete guide erectile dysfunction](/blog/complete-guide-erectile-dysfunction), emphasizes that the medication provides the biological support for a natural response rather than a forced one.

## Important Safety and Contraindications

While understanding how ED medications actually make erections possible is helpful, it is vital to recognize that they are not safe for everyone. Because these medications affect blood flow and blood pressure, they can interact dangerously with other substances.

> **Warning:** You must never take PDE5 inhibitors if you are taking nitrates (often prescribed for chest pain) or guanylate cyclase stimulators. This combination can cause a sudden, life-threatening drop in blood pressure.

Men with unstable cardiovascular disease, a recent history of stroke or heart attack, or severe liver or kidney issues should consult a clinician before use.
